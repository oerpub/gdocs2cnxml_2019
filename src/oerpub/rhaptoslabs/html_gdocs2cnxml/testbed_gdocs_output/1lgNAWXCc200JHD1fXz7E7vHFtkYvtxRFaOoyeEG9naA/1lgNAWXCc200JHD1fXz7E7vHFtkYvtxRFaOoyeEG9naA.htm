Unit: Living and non-living things

Key conc[a]epts:

  1. movement - the action of moving
  2. respiration - breathing (exchanging gases)
  3. sensitivity - to be affected by something
  4. nutrition - receiving or supplying food
  5. excretion - to get rid of waste substances from the body
  6. reproduction - to produce offspring (babies)
  7. growth - to increase in size

Characteristics of living things

Although living things look different, they all have seven things in common. We call these things the seven life processes.

Let’s take a look at each of these …...

All living things move (movement)        

  1. Humans and animals use their bodies to move from one place to another.
  1. Plants turn towards the light.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/lululemonathletica/3908348636/

All living things exchange gasses (respiration)        (O2 sign)

  1. Humans and animals use the oxygen in the air to breathe.
  2. When plants exchange gases we call this transpiration.

All living things responds to change (sensitivity)                

  1. Humans, animals and plants respond to changes in the environment. They notice changes in their surroundings and react accordingly.
  1. When you are feeling cold, you will put on a jersey.
  2. When it becomes winter some animals hibernate.
  3. In autumn some trees’ leaves change colour.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/so_cool13/2279304682/

All living things need food (nutrition)        

  1. Food gives all living things the energy they need.
  2. Green plants produce their own food through a process called photosynthesis.
  3. Humans and animals eat these plants to get energy.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/76029035@N02/6829315557/

All living things has to get rid of waste substances (excretion)        

  1. Humans and animals have to get rid of waste substances from their bodies. They do this through sweating and by going to the toilet.
  2. Plants get rid of waste water through the process of transpiration.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/8374568@N07/3451503721/

All living things produce offspring (reproduction)        

  1. Humans and animals have babies.
  1. New plants grow from seeds.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/annikaleigh/3087585719/

All living things grow (growth)

  1. Human and animal babies grow into adults.
  2. Seedlings grow into plants.

All seven of the life processes must happen for something to be living.

The girl went back to the grave where she cried and cried. She had been sitting there a long time when a man appeared from the bush. He saw the dead tree and decided it was just what he needed to make a bow and arrows, for he was a hunter.

Setting one of his new arrows to his bow, he let fly. The first buffalo sank down dead. And the second, and the third. An hour later the sweating hunter came back to tell the father to send men to bring the meat to the village.

Key concepts:

  1. Function - the job it does
  2. Chlorophyll - a substance in leaves giving a plant its green colour
  3. Photosynthesis - the process by which a plant produces its own food
  4. Fertilisation - when male and female parts of the plant come together to make a seed
  5. Germinate - begin to grow and develop

Basic structures of flowering plants

All plants have different parts, each with its own function. Most plants have roots, stems, leaves and flowers. Many plants grow from seeds.

Let’s take a look at each part of the plant and each function that it has.

Roots                

This is the part of the plant that is normally found underground. The roots have two very important functions:

  1. The roots anchor the plant in the ground.
  2. The roots absorb water and nutrients from the soil.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/60274378@N02/5532437110/

Stems                

This part of the plant connects the roots to the rest of the plant. The stem also has two functions:

  1. The stem supports the leaves, flowers and fruit.
  2. The stem carries water and minerals to the other parts of the plant.

A growing plant stem.

http://www.flickr.com/search/?l=commderiv&mt=all&adv=1&w=all&q=plant+stem&m=text

A tomato plant stem. http://www.flickr.com/photos/22206162@N03/3686817791/

Leaves        

This part of the plant is normally green. Chlorophyll gives the leaves its green colour. Some leaves change colour during autumn.

Leaves have an extremely important function.

  1. The leaves catch the sunlight and uses it to make food for the plant. This process is known as photosynthesis.

This food gives the plant its energy. When animals and humans eat plants we get energy from the plant.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/crabchick/5809576233/

Flowers        

This is the pretty, colourful part of the plant. The flowers play a very important role in making new plants.

  1. The flowers make fruit and seeds.
  2. The flowers also make pollen that is necessary for fertilisation.
  3. The colours of the flowers attract bees and other insects. These insects carry pollen from one flower to another.

http://www.flickr.com/photos/dominiqs/137546658/

Seeds                (Picture of seeds)

This part of the plant is found inside fruit.

  1. Seeds make new plants.

What plants need to grow

So now we have germinated a seed. This new little plant needs to be kept alive. To do this we need to make sure it has a few very important things.

Humans need a few things to live — we need healthy food, we need water, and we need air.

Plants are a little different.

(A picture with each heading)

Sunlight

They need food and water, but they also need sunlight to live. Plants use sunlight as a way to make food to grow through the process of photosynthesis. This food gives the plant its energy. When animals and humans eat plants we get energy from the plant.

 

Air

Plants and animals, including humans, breathe in air. But plants use a different part of the air than we do. When we breathe, we use a part of the air called oxygen. We use the oxygen that we breathe in the air to feed the blood in our bodies.

Plants use a different part of the air, a gas called carbon dioxide. We breathe in oxygen and breathe out carbon dioxide. Plants do just the opposite — plants breathe in carbon dioxide and breathe out oxygen.

Water

Plants also need water. Some plants need more water than others. The amount of water needed by a plant depends on the type of soil the plant is planted in, the climate of the area where the plant lives as well as the type of plant.

The roots of the plant absorbs water from the soil. This water allows nutrients from the soil to be transported through the stem to the leaves, flowers, fruits and seeds. Besides sunlight, plants also use water to make food for us through photosynthesis.

Soil

Most plants grow in soil.

Providing plants with rich soil will help it receive the nutrients and minerals it needs. The three most vital nutrients that a plant can receive are nitrogen, phosphorous and potassium.

Nitrogen helps the part of the plant that grows above the ground. Phosphorous helps the plant to produce flowers and seeds, and to develop a strong root system. Potassium acts like medicine for the plant and helps it to fight off disease. It also gives it strong stems.

To make sure plants get enough of these minerals we often add some fertiliser to help enrich the soil.

Habitat

When all of these  vital elements are available in the right quantities, a specific plant will grow naturally in that area.

Because there are many different kinds of plants, they grow in many different places. The place that a plant lives in is called a habitat. There are many different types of habitats that make a home for plants.

 

Some plants grow in the hot, dry desert. The desert is a habitat. These plants do not need as much water as other types of plants. Some plants grow in forests or caves that get very little sunlight. This is a special home just made for these types of plants.

Unit: Features of animals

Key concepts:

  1. Vertebrate - animals that have a backbone (spine)
  2. Invertebrate - animals that do not have a backbone (spine)
  3. Characteristics - a quality that forms part of a persons character
  4. Warm-blooded - the blood in the body remains at a constant temperature
  5. Cold-blooded - the blood in the body has the same temperature as the surrounding temperature

Basic body plans of animals

When animals are classified the first thing we look at is whether the animal has a backbone (spine) or not.

If the animal has a backbone it is classified as a vertebrate. An example would be a dog.                (Picture of dog)

If the animal does not have a backbone it is classified as an invertebrate, for example a jellyfish.                (Picture of jellyfish)

In grade 4 we focus mainly on vertebrates. Let’s take a look!

Vertebrates are divided into 5 main categories:

  1. Mammals
  2. Birds
  3. Fish
  4. Reptiles
  5. Amphibians

In the next section we will look at these in more detail to see how they differ.

Visible differences between animals

[e]

The animals in the 5 different vertebrate groups have characteristics that distinguish them from each other.

These characteristics represent certain features the animals have and makes it possible for us to compare them and to notice the visible differences.

The following questions allow us to determine these characteristics:

  1. What are their bodies covered with?
  2. How are their babies born?
  3. Are they warm-blooded or cold-blooded?

Unit: What animals need to survive

Key concepts:

  1. Herbivore - animal that only eats plants
  2. Carnivore - animal that only eats meat
  3. Omnivore - animal that eats meat and plants
  4. Camouflage - a way of hiding by looking the same as the surroundings
  5. Predator - an animal that hunts or preys upon others
  6. Prey - an animal that is hunted or killed by another for food
  7. Indigenous - originating in a particular country

Animals need a number of things to survive. These things include food, water, shelter and even the ability to camouflage themselves.

Food        (Picture for each heading/key concept used)

All animals need food to survive.

Some animals eat parts of plants, such as nuts, berries, twigs and leaves. These animals are called herbivores. Examples of herbivores are buck, elephants and tortoises.

Other animals eat insects, mice and other small rodents, and even larger animals. These animals are called carnivores. Examples of carnivores are lions, sharks and crocodiles.

Animals that eat parts of plants as well as other animals, are called omnivores. People are omnivores, so are bears and baboons.

Water

Animals get water from puddles, streams and ponds.

Animals, and humans, need water to stay hydrated and to remain healthy. Water delivers oxygen and nutrients to different parts of the body and helps the body to function properly.

Shelter

Shelter provides the animal and its babies with protection from the weather and its enemies, such as humans and other animals.

Habitat

When all of these  vital elements are available in the right quantities, a specific animal will live naturally in that area.

In other words, habitat is the physical area where the animal lives. An animal’s natural habitat has everything it needs to live.

Camouflage

Animals use camouflage for two reasons.

Firstly, they use it to hide from predators. In other words, their camouflage helps them to hide from other animals that eat them.

Secondly, they use it to hide from their prey. In other words, when they are hunting it helps them to sneak up on other animals without being seen.

Animals are camouflaged in different ways.

Some animals hide against a background that has the same colour as they do. Others have stripes or spots that help break up their outline so that they don’t stick out against the background. Some animals look like other objects and are, therefore, not very visible.

Key concepts:

Man-made and natural shelters

Animals live in a variety of different kinds of homes from holes in the ground, to caves, nests and trees. Some animals use natural habitat for their homes. Other animals build their own home. Some animals even live in other animal's homes.

Look at the following picture of a few places where animals live.

Let’s take a look at a few shelters that animals build and the materials they use.

Nests

Nests are built by birds and other animals in trees, on the ground and even in buildings. This is used as a home for them and especially for their eggs. Nests are usually bowl-shaped and made of twigs, leaves and grass held together by mud or saliva.

(Picture of a nest)

Hives

Bees live in very large colonies. They collect nectar and pollen from flowers which is used by the queen bee to make wax in her abdomen. The queen bee uses this wax to build a hive in the ground, in trees or even in buildings. The hive is made up of many six-sided cells stuck together. She lays her eggs in these cells.

(Picture of a bee-hive showing the 6-sided cells)

Holes in the ground

Small rodents such as squirrels, rabbits and moles, dig burrows in the ground or under logs and rocks to provide them with shelter. These burrows often form a network of tunnels under ground in which these animals live.

Ants and earthworms also live in the ground.

(Picture of an animal burrowing)

Webs

Spiders spin webs from silk produced by a gland in their abdomen called the spinneret. The web isn’t only a home for the spider, it also helps the spider to catch it’s prey.

(Picture of a spider web)

              SHELL STRUCTURES

Shell structures mainly contain and/or protect the contents. A bird’s egg protects the embryo growing inside it. A car gives some protection to its passengers . A pot contains the food inside it. The glass of a light bulb contains the filament and protects it from oxygen.

                   MASS OR SOLID STRUCTURES

Mass or solid structures contains or supports by virtue of their mass. The mass of a solid dam wall holds back the water pressing against it. A brick has no framework but the slid mass of particles supports the load carried.

                   FRAME STRUCTURES

The primary function of a frame structure is support. The skeleton supports the body and provides anchoring points for muscles. The bones also acts as a  system of levers. a frame structure must be able to carry the designed load in the right positions without it collapsing or falling over.

Frames are made of structural members joined to make a framework using triangulation to achieve strength with economy of material. Suitable members are selected for the anticipated forces - pulling, pushing or twisting.
